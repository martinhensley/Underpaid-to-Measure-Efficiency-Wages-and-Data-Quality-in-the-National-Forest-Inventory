Dear Editors,

I am pleased to submit the enclosed manuscript, "Underpaid to Measure? Efficiency Wages and Data Quality in the National Forest Inventory," for consideration as an original research article in *Forest Policy and Economics*.

The paper addresses a question of direct policy relevance to national forest inventory programs: does the institutional design of the USDA Forest Inventory and Analysis (FIA) program create structural conditions under which rational field technicians would systematically approximate measurements rather than follow full protocols? Using a Shapiro-Stiglitz (1984) efficiency wage model calibrated to FIA institutional parameters — federal pay schedules, quality assurance audit rates, and competing labor market wages — I find that the no-shirking condition critical wage exceeds current GS-4 technician compensation by approximately 78% and GS-6 crew leader compensation by approximately 43% at central parameter values.

The paper makes three contributions to the forest policy literature. First, the calibrated principal-agent model provides a formal framework for evaluating specific interventions — wage adjustments, monitoring intensities, and institutional reforms — in terms of their effect on data quality incentives. Second, using 4.6 million tree observations from eight states and 94,459 paired QA/production measurements from the Yanai et al. (2023) dataset covering 24 northern states, I conduct five empirical tests. The paired comparison provides direct evidence: production crews record visual estimation (HTCD=3) for 23.1% of heights versus 5.8% for QA crews — a 4:1 ratio consistent with differential incentives across crew types. Third, the analysis demonstrates that increasing effective detection probability — through expanded quality assurance, technology-assisted monitoring, or autonomous sensing systems — is substantially more cost-effective than raising wages within the General Schedule structure.

This manuscript is part of a planned research program examining data quality incentives in public natural resource monitoring programs. Follow-up work will extend the paired measurement analysis to additional regions and quantify the propagation of measurement error into downstream applications including timber market analysis and carbon accounting.

The manuscript has not been published elsewhere and is not under consideration at another journal. All analyses use publicly available FIA DataMart data; replication code is available upon request.

I believe the paper is well suited to *Forest Policy and Economics* given the journal's focus on the intersection of economic analysis and forest management policy. FIA data quality has direct implications for the $80 billion U.S. timber economy and national carbon accounting, and the institutional design questions raised here extend to analogous monitoring programs in fisheries, environmental compliance, and agriculture.
