# The Ocular Estimate Problem: Revised Paper Outline

**Target:** Journal of Forest Economics (JFE)
**Word count target:** ~8,000 words
**Scope:** Single paper centered on empirical documentation of differential estimation with PA model as motivating framework

---

## 1. Introduction (~750 words)

- Opening: The FIA program as national timber information infrastructure ($80B/year)
- The empirical puzzle: production crews self-report ocular estimation at 4× QA crew rates
- Three contributions: (1) empirical documentation of differential estimation using paired data, (2) PA model as motivating benchmark, (3) policy recommendations
- Brief positioning in efficiency wage / moral hazard / multi-task literature
- Road map (7 sections)

## 2. Institutional Background and Theoretical Framework (~2,500 words)

- **2.1 The FIA Program** (~300 words)
  - Annual inventory; panel design; measurement protocols
  - Seasonal workforce structure (GS-4/5 term appointments)

- **2.2 Quality Assurance Structure** (~250 words)
  - Hot checks + blind checks; stated 4% QA rate
  - Compound detection probability from technician's perspective

- **2.3 The Seasonal Labor Market** (~350 words)
  - Term appointments, rehire uncertainty
  - Competing outdoor employment; geographic variation
  - End-of-season dynamics; termination barriers

- **2.4 A Motivating Model** (~1,000 words)
  - SS framework as benchmark (not literal description)
  - Setup, NSC derivation, finite-horizon caveat
  - Parameter table (Table 1)
  - Calibration: central result ($30.69, 78% gap), sensitivity to q ($22.91 at q=0.04)

- **2.5 Multi-Task Predictions and Behavioral Extensions** (~400 words)
  - Multi-task (Holmström-Milgrom): verifiability gradient, height as lowest-detection margin
  - Intrinsic motivation: Bénabou-Tirole, erosion mechanisms, crowding-out reconciliation
  - Workforce heterogeneity: career-track vs terminal seasonals, career ladder, self-selection

## 3. Empirical Analysis (~2,500 words)

- **3.1 Data** (~400 words)
  - Yanai et al. (2023) paired dataset: 94,459 trees, 24 states, 2011-2016
  - Sample construction (live-tree filter, valid HTCD); no Georgia
  - Production vs QA crew institutional differences

- **3.2 Height Method Codes** (~800 words)
  - Headline: 23.1% vs 5.8% (4:1 ratio); species breakdown
  - Cross-tabulation (6,402 vs 1,010); Figure 2 (HTCD by month with Wilson CIs)
  - Seasonal null (p=0.54); state-level variation
  - Authorized estimation caveat

- **3.3 Corroborating Evidence** (~700 words)
  - Height/diameter discrepancy distributions (3.31 ft vs 0.055 in.)
  - Accuracy by method (HTCD=1 vs HTCD=3)
  - Allometric conformity null and implications for monitoring design
  - Remeasurement anomalies (6:1 ratio, conifer p=0.98)

- **3.4 Discussion** (~600 words)
  - Identification: institutional differences broadly, not incentives in isolation
  - Alternative mechanisms (selection, training, task framing, workload)
  - Seasonal null interpretation
  - Policy robustness under multiple mechanisms

## 4. Implications for Timber Markets and Data Governance (~600 words)

- Systematic error and volume estimation bias
- Downstream effects: stumpage pricing, carbon accounting, forest health
- Data governance parallels (fisheries, environmental compliance)
- Implications for empirical research using FIA data

## 5. Policy Recommendations (~700 words)

- Wage adjustments: NSC at central parameters vs GS scale
- Increasing detection probability: expanded QA, tech-assisted monitoring, automated checks
- Institutional redesign: extended appointments, performance feedback, career ladders
- Autonomous sensing as structural alternative

## 6. Limitations (~450 words)

- Geographic/temporal scope; self-reported HTCD
- Selection vs incentives; authorized estimation fraction
- Binary effort; effort cost parameterization
- Crew composition and self-selection
- Generalizability

## 7. Conclusion (~430 words)

- Lead with empirical findings (4:1 ratio, height-diameter asymmetry)
- Model as interpretive framework for policy
- Three key takeaways: structural failure, monitoring > wages, HTCD-based monitoring
- Broader applicability to monitoring programs
